1. What this extension is, and the problem it solves
----------------------------------------------------

AstroBin is the largest database of astrophotography in the world. Every image
lists how long it was exposed - its integration time - often as a headline
figure like "42 hours." Newcomers and veterans alike use that number as a proxy
for "how good" or "how deep" an image is.

It is a deeply misleading proxy.

Integration time only tells you how long the shutter was open. It says nothing
about how much light was actually collected, which is what determines how deep an
image really goes. Two images of the same target, both at "10 hours," can differ
by more than a factor of ten in real signal collected, because the amount of
light gathered depends on:

  • Aperture - a 200 mm telescope collects roughly seven times the light of a
  75 mm refractor at the same exposure time.
  • Sky brightness - an hour under a pristine rural sky is worth many hours
  from a light-polluted city.
  • Sensor and filter throughput - quantum efficiency, filter transmission,
  and filter bandwidth all change how much usable signal lands on the sensor.
  • Whether it is broadband or narrowband, and how the colour sensor's Bayer
  matrix splits the light.

So "hours" is the wrong currency. It is like comparing two journeys by how long
the engine ran, ignoring the speed of the vehicle.

AstroBin Depth Translator replaces that broken comparison. It adds a small
panel to any AstroBin image and answers two questions:

1. How deep is this image, really? - expressed as a single, gear-independent
   number on a fixed reference setup, so any two images become directly
   comparable at a glance.
2. How long would it take me to match it? - the equivalent exposure time on
   your own telescope, camera, filters, and sky, broken down per band.

It is not a judgement of artistic quality, framing, processing, or seeing. It
estimates one physical thing: the depth of the underlying data.

2. The central idea: depth per unit area of sky
-----------------------------------------------

The quantity that actually matters for deep-sky imaging is **signal-to-noise
ratio (SNR)** - how far the faint target signal rises above the random noise in
the image. More integration, more aperture, and darker skies all increase SNR.
"Depth" in this document means SNR on the faint, extended structure of a target
(nebulosity, galaxy arms, tidal streams).

The key modelling decision is to measure SNR per unit area of sky - per fixed
patch of the sky as seen from Earth (for example, per square arcsecond) - rather
than per pixel.

Why per area and not per pixel? Because per-pixel SNR depends on how finely you
sample the sky, which is an arbitrary choice of optics and sensor, not a property
of how much real data you collected:

  • A long focal length spreads a patch of sky across many pixels. Each pixel
  receives fewer photons, so per-pixel SNR drops - but you have more pixels.
  • A short focal length crams the same patch onto fewer pixels. Each pixel gets
  more photons, so per-pixel SNR rises - but you have fewer pixels.

In both cases the total light collected from that patch of sky is the same. If
you measure SNR over a fixed area of sky (summing or averaging across whatever
pixels happen to cover it), the arbitrary sampling choice cancels out, and you
are left with a number that reflects genuine data depth. This is exactly how
experienced imagers think when they say a fast, small scope and a slow, large
scope "go equally deep per unit time" - they are comparing area-normalised SNR.

This single choice is what makes the whole comparison fair and is the foundation
of everything below.

----------------------------------------------------------------------


3. The physics, from photons to a comparable number
---------------------------------------------------

Consider one fixed patch of sky containing part of the target. Over an exposure
of duration t, the telescope collects photons from two sources: the target and
the sky background.


Signal

The number of target photons detected from that patch is proportional to:

    S  ∝  A · Σ_target · QE · T · Δλ · t

where:

  • A = collecting area of the aperture (∝ the square of the aperture diameter D)
  • Σ_target = surface brightness of the target in that patch (a property of the
  object, identical for everyone imaging it)
  • QE = quantum efficiency of the sensor (fraction of photons detected)
  • T = transmission of the filter (fraction of light passed)
  • Δλ = bandwidth - the width of the spectral window the filter admits
  • t = exposure time


Noise

Deep-sky imaging from any real sky is almost always background-limited: the
dominant source of noise is the random (Poisson) fluctuation in the sky-glow
photons, not the camera's read noise or dark current. For a Poisson process the
noise equals the square root of the number of events, so the noise from the sky
background in that patch is:

    N  ∝  √( A · Σ_sky · QE · T · Δλ · t )

where Σ_sky is the sky surface brightness (how bright the background glow is).


Signal-to-noise ratio

Dividing signal by noise:

    SNR = S / N  ∝  Σ_target · √( A · QE · T · Δλ · t / Σ_sky )

Two things fall out of this immediately:

1. The target's surface brightness Σ_target is a constant for everyone
   photographing the same object. When we compare two images of the same target,
   it cancels. That is why the target itself never appears in any of the
   extension's formulas - and why the tool works without knowing what the object
   is.

2. SNR grows as the square root of (aperture area × throughput × time ÷ sky).
   Because area ∝ D², the square root of area is ∝ D, the aperture diameter. This
   is the origin of every term in the engine.

We define depth as the part of SNR that depends on the gear, the sky, and the
time (everything except the cancelling target term):

    depth  ∝  D · √( QE · T · Δλ · t / Σ_sky )      (broadband)

----------------------------------------------------------------------


4. The depth formulas
---------------------

It is convenient to separate the rate at which depth accumulates from the time
itself. Since depth grows as √t, we write:

    depth = coeff · √t

where coeff is the per-rig "depth rate" - how fast a given telescope, sensor,
filter and sky build up signal-to-noise. It does not depend on time.

The two regimes (broadband and emission line) differ in how bandwidth enters.


Broadband (luminance, R, G, B)

For broadband light, both the target and the sky span the filter's band, so a
wider band collects proportionally more of both. The signal grows with Δλ and the
sky noise grows with √Δλ, leaving a net √Δλ benefit:

    coeff_broadband = D · √( QE · T · Δλ / Σ_sky )

A wider broadband filter therefore goes deeper per unit time (more total photons),
which is why luminance - the widest band - dominates broadband depth.


Emission line (Hα, OIII, SII)

For a narrowband emission line, essentially all of the target's light sits inside
a very thin spectral line. As long as the filter is at least as wide as the line,
narrowing the filter further does not cut the target signal - but it does
cut the sky background that leaks through. So for a line, the signal is
independent of bandwidth, while the sky noise still grows with √Δλ:

    coeff_line = D · √( QE · T / (Σ_sky · Δλ) )

Here a narrower filter goes deeper, because it rejects more skyglow and
moonlight while preserving the line. This is the whole reason 3 nm narrowband
filters outperform 7 nm filters under bright skies.


Sky flux

The sky surface brightness Σ_sky is derived from the sky-quality measure SQM
(magnitudes per square arcsecond) using the standard magnitude-to-flux relation:

    Σ_sky  ∝  10^(−0.4 · SQM)

A higher SQM means a darker sky and exponentially less background flux. Each
1.0 mag/arcsec² of darker sky is about 2.5× less sky flux.


Matching an image

To find how long your rig needs to reach the same depth as an image, set your
depth equal to the image's depth and solve for time:

    depth_image = coeff_you · √(your_time)
    ⟹  your_time = ( depth_image / coeff_you )²

----------------------------------------------------------------------


5. The translator: converting one rig's time to another's
---------------------------------------------------------

Combining the two relations above, the time your rig needs to match an image is:

    your_time = their_time · ( coeff_them / coeff_you )²

Expanding the coefficient ratio for broadband gives the intuitive, fully explicit
translator formula the extension shows on hover:

    your_time = their_time
              · (D_them / D_you)²            ← aperture: collecting-area ratio
              · (QE·T·Δλ)_them /(QE·T·Δλ)_you ← sensor + filter efficiency
              · (Σ_sky,you / Σ_sky,them)      ← your sky vs. their sky

For an emission line the bandwidth term flips (it divides rather than multiplies,
per the line coefficient), and a Bayer-area term is added for colour sensors
(Section 8).

Each factor has a plain-language meaning:

  • Aperture ratio (squared): a scope with half the aperture needs four times
  the exposure, because collecting area scales with the square of diameter.
  • Sensor + filter efficiency: a more sensitive sensor or higher-throughput
  filter shortens the required time proportionally.
  • Sky ratio: imaging from a brighter sky multiplies the time needed; from a
  darker sky, it divides it.

Crucially, the target's brightness cancels (it is the same object), so the
translator never needs to know what the object is.

6. Why focal length and pixel size do not appear
------------------------------------------------

A frequent and reasonable question: *surely pixel size, focal length, and
f-ratio matter?* For framing and resolution, yes. For per-area depth, no - and
this is a direct consequence of the per-area choice in Section 2.

  • Focal length changes the image scale: it determines how much sky lands on
  each pixel, i.e. how big the target appears. It does not change how many target
  photons the aperture collects from a given patch of sky.
  • Pixel size changes how that fixed bucket of photons is divided among
  pixels. Smaller pixels split it into more, fainter pixels; larger pixels into
  fewer, brighter ones. Summed over a fixed area of sky, the total is unchanged.
  • F-ratio (focal length ÷ aperture) is famous for setting per-pixel exposure
  speed - the "f-ratio myth" debate in astrophotography. Per pixel, a faster
  f-ratio is brighter. But per unit area of sky, only the aperture (total light
  grasp) and throughput matter. Two scopes of equal aperture reach the same
  per-area depth in the same time regardless of f-ratio; the faster one simply
  packs that depth into a wider field at coarser resolution.

So the reference rig and the engine deliberately ignore focal length and pixel
size for the depth calculation. They are not thrown away, though: the extension
separately compares your pixel scale (computed as 206.265 × pixel_µm ÷ focal_mm,
in arcseconds per pixel) to the image's, and reports whether your setup would
sample the target more finely or more coarsely. That is a statement about
resolution, kept distinct from depth.

----------------------------------------------------------------------


7. Bands, not filters
---------------------

Real images are built from several channels. The extension reasons in terms of
bands rather than individual filter brands, and never mixes the two physical
regimes into one number.

  • Broadband, mono camera: each of L, R, G, B is captured separately. The tool
  reports the time per channel and a broadband subtotal (their sum, since they are
  shot one after another).
  • Broadband, colour (OSC) camera: R, G, B are captured simultaneously in one
  exposure through the Bayer matrix, so broadband is reported as a single combined
  number.
  • Narrowband: each emission line (Hα, OIII, SII) is treated on its own, with
  its own depth and its own required time, because the lines are physically
  independent and a filter that is great for one may be useless for another.

When an image combines broadband and narrowband data, the two are **never blended
into a single depth scalar** - adding an SNR figure from a 300 nm luminance band
to one from a 3 nm OIII line would be physically meaningless. They are shown as
separate groups. (A total clock time across all bands is still meaningful and is
shown as the headline - see Section 11 - because time is additive even when depth
is not.)

To combine the broadband channels into one broadband depth, their individual
depths are added in quadrature (root-sum-square), which is the correct way to
combine independent signal-to-noise contributions:

    depth_broadband_total = √( depth_L² + depth_R² + depth_G² + depth_B² )

8. Colour sensors and the Bayer matrix
--------------------------------------

A one-shot-colour (OSC) sensor places a red, green, or blue micro-filter over each
pixel in a repeating mosaic (the Bayer matrix: 25% red, 50% green, 25% blue). This
has a large effect on narrowband performance that the model accounts for
explicitly.

  • For an emission line on a colour sensor, only the pixels whose colour
  matches the line actually detect it. Hα and SII (deep red) are seen mainly by
  the 25% of pixels that are red; OIII (blue-green) by roughly the 50% that are
  green (and some blue). The model multiplies the native pixel QE by this **Bayer
  area fraction** (0.25 for Hα/SII, 0.50 for OIII), because only that fraction of
  the sensor area is collecting the line. This is why a colour camera with a
  dual-band filter is dramatically slower on narrowband than a mono camera with
  dedicated filters - often by 4× or more - and the tool will flag truly
  impractical cases.
  • For broadband on a colour sensor, the Bayer loss is already baked into the
  effective broadband quantum efficiency (qe_lum) stored for that camera, so no
  additional area factor is applied - applying it twice would double-count.

For mono sensors the Bayer fraction is 1.0 throughout (every pixel sees every
band, through whichever filter is mounted).

9. Sky brightness: SQM, Bortle, and the Moon
--------------------------------------------

Sky brightness enters every formula through Σ_sky, and the extension determines it
in order of preference:

1. Measured SQM if the image lists one - the most accurate input, used
   directly.
2. Bortle class if only that is given - converted to a representative SQM via
   a standard lookup table (for example Bortle 4 ≈ 21.2, Bortle 6 ≈ 19.9, Bortle 8
   ≈ 18.4 mag/arcsec²). This is an approximation, and the confidence score is
   reduced accordingly (Section 13).
3. A dark-sky default (SQM ≈ 21.0) if nothing is listed, clearly noted.


Narrowband and skyglow

Narrowband filters reject the vast majority of light pollution and moonlight,
because city lighting and the Moon emit little at the exact wavelengths of the
target lines. A bright broadband sky therefore does not translate into a
bright narrowband sky. The engine enforces a narrowband sky floor (treating
the narrowband sky as no worse than roughly SQM 21.3 regardless of Bortle), so it
will not wrongly punish narrowband imaging from a city. This matches the lived
experience that narrowband works well even under heavy light pollution.


Moonlight

The Moon brightens the broadband sky only. The extension models moonlight as
an additive reduction in the effective SQM that grows non-linearly toward full
Moon (using the square of the illuminated fraction as a coarse proxy, since
moonlit sky brightness rises steeply near full). It is applied to broadband bands
only; narrowband is left on its floor. The Moon contribution is off by default
and exposed as a checkbox, because most serious deep-sky work is planned around
the Moon; ticking it factors in the image's listed lunar phase.

10. The reference rig (the universal yardstick)
-----------------------------------------------

To make any two images comparable regardless of who shot them, the extension
expresses every image's depth as the equivalent time on a single fixed
reference rig - the "reference-hours" figure. The reference is:

  • 203 mm (8") aperture - a common, mid-range deep-sky aperture.
  • An ASI2600MM-class monochrome sensor, with realistic per-band quantum
  efficiencies.
  • 7 nm narrowband filters and standard broadband filters at typical
  transmissions.
  • Bortle 4 sky (SQM ≈ 21.2), no Moon.

Because focal length and pixel size do not affect per-area depth (Section 6), the
reference does not need to specify them. The reference is a tunable constant: it
is the yardstick, not a claim that everyone should own this rig. Its only purpose
is to give every image a number on the same scale, so that "31 reference-hours"
means the same depth whether the image was shot on a 60 mm refractor or a metre-
class telescope.

----------------------------------------------------------------------


11. The headline number: total shooting time
--------------------------------------------

The large headline figure is the total reference-hours across all bands - the
whole time the reference scope would spend shooting to reach this image's depth in
every band it contains: the combined broadband group plus each narrowband line,
summed.

This is deliberately a sum of clock time, not a single depth. As noted in Section
7, depths across different band kinds cannot be added, but the time spent
acquiring them can - and "how long in total" is the most intuitive headline for
"how big a project is this." Beneath the headline, a breakdown line splits it into
its broadband and narrowband portions (for example "6 min broadband + 31 h 30 m
narrowband"), so the dominant cost is always visible.

The per-rig table below the headline mirrors this: it lists each band's time on
your gear, a broadband subtotal, a narrowband subtotal, and a grand total -
indented detail rows under flush subtotals, so the hierarchy reads at a glance.

----------------------------------------------------------------------


12. Multi-line filters: dual-band vs. filter sets
-------------------------------------------------

A subtlety that the extension gets right, and which is easy to get wrong:
narrowband total time depends on how the lines are captured.

  • A true dual- or tri-band filter (for example an Optolong L-eXtreme, which
  passes Hα and OIII through one piece of glass) captures all of its lines **in a
  single exposure, simultaneously.** The total narrowband time is therefore the
  time of the slowest line it covers - the others come "for free" in the same
  sub-exposures. The engine groups the lines served by one such filter and takes
  the maximum.

  • A filter set or bundle (for example an SHO set of separate 7 nm Hα, OIII,
  and SII filters) is three separate filters, shot one at a time, sequentially.
  Their times add up. The engine sums them.

Confusing the two understates a set's real cost dramatically. For example, with a
sequential SHO set, 8 hours of Hα plus 25 hours of OIII is a 33-hour project - not
a 25-hour one. The extension distinguishes the cases with a set flag, inferred
from the filter being a recognised set/bundle, or carrying three or more emission
lines (no single interference filter usefully passes Hα, OIII, and SII at once),
or having "set"/"bundle" in its name. Dual/tri-band filters default to the
simultaneous (max) treatment; sets to the sequential (sum) treatment.

13. The confidence score
------------------------

Every estimate is only as good as its inputs, so the panel shows a confidence
badge with a hover breakdown of four checks:

1. Image camera QE - was the sensor matched to a known quantum efficiency, or
   estimated?
2. Sky brightness - a measured SQM is fully solid; a Bortle class is a good
   approximation (partial credit); nothing listed is a gap.
3. Image filters - were the bandwidths and transmissions identified, or
   assumed from standard specs?
4. Your rig specs - is your own gear fully entered?

Each check contributes a score: 1.0 for a solid input, 0.75 for a reasonable
approximation (such as sky-from-Bortle), 0.0 for missing. The overall rating is
the total score divided by the number of checks:

  • ≥ 0.95 → High
  • ≥ 0.80 → Medium-High
  • ≥ 0.50 → Medium
  • otherwise → Low

So an image with everything known except a measured SQM (Bortle given instead)
lands at Medium-High; with a real SQM it reaches High; with no sky data at
all it drops to Medium. The badge is colour-coded and the hover lists each
check with a ✓ (solid), ≈ (approximate), or ✗ (missing) marker, so the user always
knows how much to trust the number.

14. A fully worked example
--------------------------

Suppose an image was shot on a 203 mm scope with a mono ASI2600MM-class sensor
under a Bortle 4 sky, with 12 h of luminance and 6 h of Hα through a 7 nm filter,
and you want to know the time on the same class of rig.

For luminance (broadband), the coefficient is coeff = D · √(QE·T·Δλ / Σ_sky).
Because your rig matches the reference closely, your luminance coefficient is
near the reference's, so your luminance time comes out near the original 12 h, and
R/G/B each near their listed times. Summed, that is the broadband subtotal.

For Hα (a line), coeff = D · √(QE·T / (Σ_sky · Δλ)). With the same aperture,
sensor, 7 nm filter and sky, your Hα time again lands near the original 6 h.

The headline reference-hours is the sum of the combined broadband group and the
Hα line - the whole shooting project on the yardstick rig. Because every input is
known except the sky is given as Bortle rather than a measured SQM, the confidence
reads Medium-High.

Now change one thing: image the same target from your back garden at Bortle 7
instead of Bortle 4. The sky ratio term Σ_sky,you / Σ_sky,them rises sharply for
the broadband channels (skyglow is far worse), multiplying your broadband time
several-fold - while the Hα time barely moves, because the narrowband sky floor
shields it from the light pollution. The panel would show the broadband ballooning
and the narrowband almost unchanged, exactly as a real imager would experience.

15. Assumptions and limitations
-------------------------------

The model is a physically-grounded estimate, not a laboratory measurement. Its
main simplifying assumptions:

  • Background-limited imaging. It assumes sky-background noise dominates read
  noise and dark current. This holds for typical deep-sky sub-exposures from a
  real sky, but breaks down for very short subs, extremely narrow filters under
  truly dark skies, or sensors with unusually high read noise. Two cameras with
  identical QE but different read noise are treated as equal.
  • Read noise and dark current are not modelled. Only QE, transmission,
  bandwidth, aperture, sky, and time enter.
  • Filter passes the whole line. For emission lines it assumes the filter is at
  least as wide as the target line, so narrowing rejects only sky, not signal.
  • Quantum efficiencies are catalogue/peak values, from a built-in table or
  estimated when unknown; AstroBin does not publish QE.
  • Filter transmission and bandwidth are sometimes assumed. AstroBin publishes
  bandwidth for some filters but never transmission, so standard per-type values
  are used and disclosed in the notes.
  • Surface brightness is uniform within a patch. The per-area treatment is an
  average; it does not model how SNR varies across bright cores vs. faint outskirts.
  • It estimates depth, not quality. Framing, focus, guiding, seeing,
  processing skill, and artistic merit are entirely outside its scope.
  • Outputs are only as good as the metadata each image's author entered on
  AstroBin. Missing or wrong gear/sky data propagates into the estimate, which is
  why the confidence score exists.

16. Where the numbers come from (data sources)
----------------------------------------------

  • The image's acquisition card on the page is read directly from the rendered
  AstroBin image viewer: integration time per channel, the equipment list, and the
  sky/pixel-scale details in the astrometry header. Every selector has a text-based
  fallback so a markup change degrades gracefully rather than breaking.
  • AstroBin's own public equipment API is queried (the same data the website
  itself loads) to fill in aperture and focal length for telescopes, and bandwidth
  and sensor details for cameras and filters, when a piece of gear is not in the
  built-in table. Results are cached locally so the same item is never requested
  twice.
  • A built-in equipment table supplies the specifications AstroBin does not
  publish - principally sensor quantum efficiencies and filter transmissions - for
  common cameras, scopes, and filters, plus the user's own gear. It is editable,
  and any gear you enter is remembered privately in your browser.

No scraping of AstroBin's protected data is performed; only the public API and the
already-rendered page content are used.

----------------------------------------------------------------------


17. Software architecture
-------------------------

The extension is a Manifest V3 Chrome extension with no build step - plain
JavaScript, injected as content scripts on AstroBin image pages.

  • equipment.js - the data layer: the camera/scope/filter tables, the
  Bortle→SQM table, the reference rig, and the physical constants (Bayer
  fractions, moon model, narrowband sky floor, practical-time limit). Exposes
  window.ADT_DATA.
  • engine.js - the calculation engine: the per-area depth model, the
  coefficient functions, the translator, the reference-hours and subtotal
  computations, and the confidence score. Pure functions, independently testable
  in Node. Exposes window.ADT_ENGINE.
  • parser.js - reads the rendered AstroBin page into an engine-ready image
  object (channels, equipment, sky), with robust fallbacks. Exposes
  window.ADT_PARSER.
  • content.js - orchestration and UI: builds the panel, the inline gear
  settings (type-ahead search backed by the AstroBin API, raw-spec entry, private
  custom gear, multi-filter chips), draws the results table, and watches the
  single-page app for navigation so the panel re-renders on each image.
  • panel.css - styling that mirrors AstroBin's dark theme.
  • background.js - a minimal service worker that exists only to relay the
  optional, off-by-default analytics events (Section 18).

The scripts load in dependency order (data → engine → parser → content) and
communicate through the three window.ADT_* globals. Editing any file requires
reloading the unpacked extension and refreshing the AstroBin tab to re-inject the
updated content script.

----------------------------------------------------------------------


18. Privacy and analytics
-------------------------

The extension is built to run entirely in the browser. It reads only the AstroBin
page you are viewing and AstroBin's public equipment API; it accesses no other
sites and reads no browsing history. Your gear settings and the cached equipment
specs are stored locally in Chrome's storage.local and never leave your device.
It requires no account or login.

There is an optional, off-by-default anonymous analytics toggle. Only if you
explicitly tick it (and only if the developer has configured a collection
endpoint, which ships empty) does it send anonymous events - a random install ID,
which gear and settings are used, and error messages - to help improve the tool.
It never includes your name, your AstroBin account, image URLs, or any personal
data, and can be turned off at any time. This honest opt-in design was a
deliberate choice over any hidden or pre-ticked collection, both because it is the
right thing to do and because deceptive data collection is prohibited by the
Chrome Web Store and would destroy trust with a technical audience.

19. Glossary of symbols
-----------------------

  • D - aperture diameter (mm). Collecting area A ∝ D².
  • QE - quantum efficiency; fraction of incident photons detected (0–1).
  • T - filter transmission; fraction of light passed (0–1).
  • Δλ - filter bandwidth (nm); the width of the spectral window.
  • Σ_target - target surface brightness; cancels between images of the same object.
  • Σ_sky - sky surface brightness (flux); Σ_sky ∝ 10^(−0.4 · SQM).
  • SQM - sky quality measure, magnitudes per square arcsecond; higher = darker.
  • t - exposure (integration) time.
  • coeff - per-rig depth rate; depth = coeff · √t.
  • depth - gear/sky/time-dependent part of SNR per unit area of sky.
  • Bayer fraction - share of a colour sensor's pixels that detect a given line
  (≈0.25 Hα/SII, ≈0.50 OIII; 1.0 for mono).
  • reference-hours - an image's depth expressed as time on the fixed reference rig.
  • pixel scale - arcseconds per pixel = 206.265 × pixel_µm ÷ focal_mm; a
  resolution measure, not a depth measure.
